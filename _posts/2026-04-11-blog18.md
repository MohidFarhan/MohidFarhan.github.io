## Introduction

In the preceding two blogs, we derived the fluid equations for Cold Dark Matter (CDM), a pressureless, collisionless dust that clusters under gravity. We obtained the continuity equation (Blog 17, equation 47) governing mass conservation, and the Euler equation (Blog 18, equation 78) governing momentum conservation. Together, they describe how structure grows in the matter sector.

But CDM is not the only player. Our universe is dominated by **Dark Energy** (DE), a mysterious component with negative pressure that drives cosmic acceleration. Unlike CDM, dark energy does not cluster freely; its pressure resists gravitational collapse, and its equation of state $$w = P/\rho$$ fundamentally alters the perturbation equations.

Today, we derive the continuity and Euler equations for a generic perfect fluid with pressure $$P \neq 0$$, specializing to dark energy. We will see how the negative pressure modifies the familiar results, and why, for a cosmological constant with $$w = -1$$, perturbations effectively vanish.

## The Setup: A Perfect Fluid with Pressure

Dark energy is modeled as a perfect fluid with energy-momentum tensor:

$$
T^{\mu\nu} = (\rho + P)u^\mu u^\nu + P g^{\mu\nu} \tag{80}
$$

For CDM, we set $$P = 0$$ and recovered equation (50) of Blog 18. For dark energy, we must keep the pressure terms. We characterize the fluid by its **equation of state parameter**:

$$
w \equiv \frac{P}{\rho} \tag{81}
$$

For a cosmological constant, $$w = -1$$. For dynamical dark energy (quintessence), $$w$$ may vary with time, but we treat it as approximately constant for this derivation.

We perturb both the density and pressure:

$$
\rho = \bar{\rho}(t) + \delta\rho(\mathbf{x}, t), \quad P = \bar{P}(t) + \delta P(\mathbf{x}, t) \tag{82}
$$

The background pressure is $$\bar{P} = w\bar{\rho}$$. For the perturbation, we introduce the **sound speed** $$c_s$$ defined in the rest frame of the fluid:

$$
\delta P = c_s^2 \delta\rho \tag{83}
$$

For a barotropic fluid (where pressure is a function of density only), $$c_s^2 = w$$. More generally, $$c_s$$ captures the microphysical response of the fluid to compression.

## The Continuity Equation: Conservation of Energy

We begin with the time component ($$\nu = 0$$) of the conservation law $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$, using equation (48) from Blog 18:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu 0} = \partial_\mu T^{\mu 0} + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda 0} + \Gamma^0_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} = 0 \tag{84}
$$

### Evaluating the Components

The components of $$T^{\mu\nu}$$ to first order are:

**Time-time**: Using equation (52) from Blog 17 for $$u^0$$:
  
$$
T^{00} \approx (\bar{\rho} + \bar{P})(1 + \delta + 2\phi) - \bar{P}(1+2\phi) = \bar{\rho}(1 + \delta + w\delta) = \bar{\rho}(1 + (1+w)\delta) \tag{85}
$$

**Time-space**: 
$$
T^{0i} = (\rho + P)u^0 u^i \approx (\bar{\rho} + \bar{P})\frac{v^i}{a} = \bar{\rho}(1+w)\frac{v^i}{a} \tag{86}
$$

**Space-space**: 
$$
T^{ij} = (\rho + P)u^i u^j + P g^{ij} \approx P g^{ij} = \bar{P} \cdot \frac{1}{a^2}(1+2\phi)\delta^{ij} \approx \frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}\delta^{ij} \tag{87}
$$

Note that $$T^{ij}$$ no longer vanishes at zeroth order, it carries the background pressure.

### The Divergence Terms

Expanding equation (84):

$$
\partial_0 T^{00} + \partial_i T^{i0} + \Gamma^0_{00}T^{00} + 2\Gamma^0_{0i}T^{0i} + \Gamma^0_{ij}T^{ij} + \Gamma^i_{i0}T^{00} = 0 \tag{88}
$$

We have used the symmetry of Christoffel symbols and the fact that $$T^{0i} = T^{i0}$$.

Substituting our expressions and keeping terms to first order:

**Time derivative**:
$$
\partial_0 T^{00} = \dot{\bar{\rho}} + \dot{\bar{P}} + \partial_t[(\bar{\rho}+\bar{P})\delta] \approx \dot{\bar{\rho}}(1+w) + \bar{\rho}(1+w)\dot{\delta} \tag{89}
$$
using $$\dot{\bar{P}} = w\dot{\bar{\rho}}$$ for constant $$w$$.

**Spatial divergence**:
$$
\partial_i T^{i0} = \frac{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}\theta \tag{90}
$$

**Christoffel terms**:
From Blog 17 equations (31)-(32), we have $$\Gamma^0_{00} = \dot{\phi}$$, $$\Gamma^i_{i0} = 3H - 3\dot{\phi}$$, and $$\Gamma^0_{ij} = a\dot{a}(1-2\phi)\delta_{ij} - a^2\dot{\phi}\delta_{ij}$$.

The contraction $$\Gamma^0_{ij}T^{ij}$$ contributes:
$$
\Gamma^0_{ij}T^{ij} = [a\dot{a}(1-2\phi)\delta_{ij} - a^2\dot{\phi}\delta_{ij}] \cdot \frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}\delta^{ij} = 3\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\bar{P}(1-2\phi) - 3\dot{\phi}\bar{P} \approx 3H\bar{P} \tag{91}
$$
where we dropped second-order terms involving $$\phi\bar{P}$$.

Collecting the background terms (zeroth order):
$$
\dot{\bar{\rho}} + 3H(\bar{\rho} + \bar{P}) = 0 \tag{92}
$$

This is the background continuity equation for a fluid with pressure. Since $$\bar{P} = w\bar{\rho}$$, this gives $$\dot{\bar{\rho}} = -3H(1+w)\bar{\rho}$$, yielding the familiar scaling $$\bar{\rho} \propto a^{-3(1+w)}$$. For $$w = -1$$, the density is constant (cosmological constant); for $$w = 0$$, we recover $$\bar{\rho} \propto a^{-3}$$.

### First Order Perturbations

Subtracting the background (92) from the full equation, we obtain the perturbation evolution:

$$
\bar{\rho}(1+w)\dot{\delta} + \frac{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}\theta + 3H\delta\bar{P} - 3\dot{\phi}(\bar{\rho}+\bar{P}) = 0 \tag{93}
$$

Dividing by $$\bar{\rho}(1+w)$$ and using $$\delta P = c_s^2\delta\rho = c_s^2\bar{\rho}\delta$$:

$$
\dot{\delta} + \frac{\theta}{a} + 3H\left(\frac{c_s^2 - w}{1+w}\right)\delta - 3\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{94}
$$

This is the **continuity equation for dark energy**. Compared to the CDM version (Blog 17, equation 44), we see two crucial differences:

1. **The adiabatic term**: The coefficient $$3H\left(\frac{c_s^2 - w}{1+w}\right)\delta$$ represents how pressure perturbations modify the density evolution. For a barotropic fluid with $$c_s^2 = w$$, this term vanishes. For $$c_s^2 \neq w$$, non-adiabatic pressure perturbations drive entropy production.

2. **The $$(1+w)$$ factors**: The effective inertia of the fluid is enhanced by $$(1+w)$$, making the fluid "heavier" against gravity when $$w > 0$$, or "lighter" when $$w &lt; 0$$.

For a cosmological constant ($$w = -1$$, $$c_s^2 = 0$$), the denominator diverges, indicating that the concept of density perturbations becomes ill-defined or gauge-dependent. In the Newtonian gauge, we typically set $$\delta = 0$$ for pure $$\Lambda$$CDM.

The magnitude of this effect is controlled by the sound speed $$c_s^2$$. For relativistic or scalar-field dark energy models ($$c_s^2 \approx 1$$) meaning pressure support operates efficiently across all sub-horizon scales. In such cases, dark energy remains nearly homogeneous and does not participate in structure formation.

Only in more exotic scenarios where $$c_s^2 << 1$$ can dark energy begin to cluster, partially mimicking the behavior of matter. This distinction is crucial in observational cosmology, where constraints on clustering help differentiate between dark energy models.

At this point, the contrast with CDM becomes stark. For pressureless matter, density evolves purely through gravitational infall and expansion. Dark energy, however, carries an internal response to compression. The additional term proportional to ($$c_s^2-w$$) acts as a regulator: instead of simply falling into potential wells, the fluid resists changes in its own density.

In other words, CDM collapses where dark energy pushes back. As a result, while matter builds structure, dark energy remains largely smooth, shaping the expansion of the universe rather than its clustering.

## The Euler Equation: Conservation of Momentum

Now we derive the spatial component ($$\nu = i$$) of the conservation law:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu i} = \partial_\mu T^{\mu i} + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda i} + \Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} = 0 \tag{95}
$$

### Evaluating the New Terms

The new ingredient is the pressure gradient in the spatial derivative and the Christoffel curvature term.

**Partial derivatives**:
$$
\partial_\mu T^{\mu i} = \partial_0 T^{0i} + \partial_j T^{ji} \tag{96}
$$

The time derivative term is similar to CDM but with $$(1+w)$$ factors:
$$
\partial_0 T^{0i} = \frac{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}(\dot{v}^i - Hv^i) \tag{97}
$$

The spatial derivative now contributes significantly because $$T^{ji} \neq 0$$:
$$
\partial_j T^{ji} = \partial_j \left(\frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}\delta^{ji}\right) + \partial_j \left(\delta P \cdot \frac{1}{a^2}\delta^{ji}\right) = \frac{1}{a^2}\partial^i \delta P \tag{98}
$$
(The background $$\bar{P}$$ term vanishes upon differentiation as it is spatially homogeneous.)

**Christoffel terms**:
The contraction $$\Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda i} = \Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0}T^{0i}$$ gives:
$$
(3H - 2\dot{\phi})\frac{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}v^i \approx \frac{3H\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}v^i \tag{99}
$$

The curvature term $$\Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda}$$ splits into:
$$
\Gamma^i_{00}T^{00} + \Gamma^i_{0j}T^{0j} + \Gamma^i_{j0}T^{j0} + \Gamma^i_{jk}T^{jk} \tag{100}
$$

Using $$\Gamma^i_{00} = \frac{1}{a^2}\partial^i\phi$$ and $$T^{00} \approx \bar{\rho}(1 + (1+w)\delta)$$:
$$
\Gamma^i_{00}T^{00} \approx \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a^2}\partial^i\phi \tag{101}
$$

The terms with $$\Gamma^i_{0j} = H\delta^i_j$$ give:
$$
2H\delta^i_j \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}v^j = \frac{2H\bar{\rho}(1+w)}{a}v^i \tag{102}
$$

Finally, the spatial curvature term $$\Gamma^i_{jk}T^{jk}$$ involves:
$$
\Gamma^i_{jk} = \delta^i_j\partial_k\phi + \delta^i_k\partial_j\phi - \delta_{jk}\partial^i\phi \tag{103}
$$

Contracting with $$T^{jk} = \frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}\delta^{jk}$$:
$$
\Gamma^i_{jk}T^{jk} = \frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}(\partial^i\phi + \partial^i\phi - 3\partial^i\phi) = -\frac{\bar{P}}{a^2}\partial^i\phi \tag{104}
$$

### Assembling the Equation

Combining all terms in (95) and multiplying by $$\frac{a}{\bar{\rho}(1+w)}$$:

From (97): $$\dot{v}^i - Hv^i$$

From (99): $$+3Hv^i$$

From (101) and (104): $$\frac{1}{a(1+w)}(\partial^i\phi - w\partial^i\phi) = \frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi$$

From (102): $$+2Hv^i$$

From (98): $$+\frac{1}{\bar{\rho}(1+w)a}\partial^i\delta P = \frac{c_s^2}{a(1+w)}\partial^i\delta$$

Summing the Hubble terms and rearranging:

$$
\dot{v}^i + \frac{\dot{a}}{a}v^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi - \frac{1}{\bar{\rho}(1+w)a}\partial^i\delta P \tag{105}
$$

Substituting $$\delta P = c_s^2\bar{\rho}\delta$$:

$$
\boxed{\dot{v}^i + Hv^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi - \frac{c_s^2}{a(1+w)}\partial^i\delta} \tag{106}
$$

This is the **Euler equation for dark energy**. The left-hand side is identical to CDM (Blog 18, equation 73), but the right-hand side now includes the **pressure gradient** term. This term provides a restoring force that opposes gravitational collapse. For large $$c_s^2$$ (stiff fluid), pressure prevents clustering; for $$c_s^2 = 0$$ (dust), we recover the CDM limit.

This equation makes the physical role of pressure explicit. The first term on the right-hand side is gravitational acceleration, identical to the CDM case. The second term is a pressure gradient force, opposing motion toward overdense regions.

For CDM, this term vanishes, and particles freely fall into potential wells. For dark energy, however, this term acts as a restoring force, suppressing velocity growth and preventing efficient clustering. The larger the sound speed, the stronger this suppression.

Taking the divergence and defining $$\theta = \partial_i v^i$$:

$$
\dot{\theta} + H\theta = -\frac{\nabla^2\phi}{a} - \frac{c_s^2}{a(1+w)}\nabla^2\delta \tag{107}
$$

Or in Fourier space:

$$
\dot{\theta} + H\theta = \frac{k^2}{a}\phi - \frac{c_s^2 k^2}{a(1+w)}\delta \tag{108}
$$

The $$c_s^2 k^2$$ term acts as a "pressure support" term, suppressing perturbations at small scales (large $$k$$) for fluids with non-zero sound speed. This is the Jeans stabilization mechanism.

## The Cosmological Constant Limit

For a pure cosmological constant, $$w = -1$$ and $$c_s^2 = 0$$. The equations become singular in the form presented above. However, taking the limit $$w \to -1$$ carefully:

1. The background density $$\bar{\rho}$$ becomes constant.
2. The continuity equation (94) implies $$\delta$$ becomes undefined or gauge-dependent; we set $$\delta = 0$$ in the Newtonian gauge.
3. The Euler equation (106) has $$(1+w) \to 0$$ in the denominator of the pressure term, but since $$c_s^2 = 0$$ for $$\Lambda$$, the term remains finite (zero).
4. The velocity $$v^i$$ redshifts away as $$v \propto a^{-1}$$ due to the Hubble drag, leaving only the background.

Thus, dark energy perturbations are negligible for $$w \approx -1$$, and the fluid remains smooth, driving acceleration through its negative pressure rather than participating in structure formation.

## Summary

We have generalized our fluid equations to include pressure, deriving the continuity and Euler equations for dark energy:

**Continuity:**
$$
\dot{\delta} + \frac{\theta}{a} + 3H\left(\frac{c_s^2 - w}{1+w}\right)\delta - 3\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{109}
$$

**Euler:**
$$
\dot{v}^i + Hv^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi - \frac{c_s^2}{a(1+w)}\partial^i\delta \tag{110}
$$

These equations reveal the competition between gravity (the $$\phi$$ terms), pressure support (the $$c_s^2$$ terms), and cosmic expansion (the $$H$$ terms). For $$w = 0$$ and $$c_s^2 = 0$$, they reduce exactly to the CDM equations of Blogs 17 and 18. For $$w = -1$$, perturbations vanish, leaving a smooth, accelerating background.

In the next blog, we will couple these equations to Einstein's field equations, closing the system and deriving the Poisson equation that links the gravitational potential $$\phi$$ to the matter and energy density contrasts. The full machinery of structure formation will then be complete.
