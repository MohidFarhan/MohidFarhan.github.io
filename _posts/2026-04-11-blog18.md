## Introduction

In our last exploration, we derived the continuity equation—the statement that mass is conserved as it flows through expanding spacetime. We watched how the density contrast $$\delta$$ evolves due to the convergence of fluid flow and the stretching of spacetime itself. But continuity alone cannot build structure; it tells us only that matter redistributes, not *why* it moves.

Today, we derive the **Euler equation** for Cold Dark Matter. If the continuity equation is the accounting of mass, the Euler equation is the physics of force. It tells us how gravitational potentials accelerate fluid elements, how the cosmic expansion dampens peculiar velocities, and how momentum conservation shapes the cosmic web.

We proceed from where we stood before, reusing the Newtonian gauge metric, the 4-velocity components, and the Christoffel symbols we painstakingly derived. Our goal is the spatial component of the conservation law $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$.

## The Setup: Spatial Components of Conservation

The conservation of energy-momentum in General Relativity is expressed through the covariant divergence:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0 \tag{48}
$$

In Blog 17, we examined the temporal component ($$\nu = 0$$) and obtained the continuity equation. Now we set $$\nu = i$$, where $$i$$ represents the three spatial indices ($$1, 2, 3$$). This gives us the evolution of momentum density—the Euler equation.

Expanding the covariant derivative, we have:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu i} = \partial_\mu T^{\mu i} + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda i} + \Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} = 0 \tag{49}
$$

For CDM, the energy-momentum tensor is that of a pressureless perfect fluid:

$$
T^{\mu\nu} = \rho u^\mu u^\nu \tag{50}
$$

where $$\rho = \bar{\rho}(1 + \delta)$$ is the total density and $$u^\mu$$ is the 4-velocity.

## Evaluating the Terms

Let us compute each piece of equation (49) systematically, keeping terms only to first order in the perturbations.

### The Partial Derivative Term $$\partial_\mu T^{\mu i}$$

This splits into time and space parts:

$$
\partial_\mu T^{\mu i} = \partial_0 T^{0i} + \partial_j T^{ji} \tag{51}
$$

First, consider $$T^{0i} = \rho u^0 u^i$$. From Blog 17, we established the 4-velocity components in equation (15):

$$
u^0 = 1 - \phi, \quad u^i = \frac{v^i}{a} \tag{52}
$$

Substituting these:

$$
T^{0i} = \rho (1 - \phi)\frac{v^i}{a} \approx \frac{\rho v^i}{a} \tag{53}
$$

Here we drop the product $$\phi v^i$$ as it is second order (both $$\phi$$ and $$v^i$$ are first-order small quantities). To linear order, $$\rho \approx \bar{\rho}$$ in the perturbation term, so:

$$
T^{0i} \approx \frac{\bar{\rho} v^i}{a} \tag{54}
$$

Now taking the time derivative:

$$
\partial_0 T^{0i} = \partial_t\left(\frac{\bar{\rho} v^i}{a}\right) = \frac{\dot{\bar{\rho}}v^i}{a} + \frac{\bar{\rho}\dot{v}^i}{a} - \frac{\dot{a}\bar{\rho}v^i}{a^2} = \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\left(\frac{\dot{\bar{\rho}}}{\bar{\rho}}v^i + \dot{v}^i - Hv^i\right) \tag{55}
$$

Using the background continuity equation $$\dot{\bar{\rho}}/\bar{\rho} = -3H$$ from equation (37) of Blog 17:

$$
\partial_0 T^{0i} = \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\left(-3Hv^i + \dot{v}^i - Hv^i\right) = \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\left(\dot{v}^i - 4Hv^i\right) \tag{56}
$$

Next, consider the spatial derivative term $$\partial_j T^{ji}$$:

$$
T^{ji} = \rho u^j u^i = \rho \frac{v^j}{a}\frac{v^i}{a} \tag{57}
$$

This is proportional to $$v^2$$, which is second order. At linear order in perturbation theory, we therefore set:

$$
\partial_j T^{ji} \approx 0 \tag{58}
$$

### The Christoffel Contraction $$\Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda i}$$

This term contracts the trace of the Christoffel symbols with the momentum density:

$$
\Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda i} = \Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0}T^{0i} + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu j}T^{ji} \tag{59}
$$

The second term involves $$T^{ji}$$, which we already established is second order, so it vanishes at linear order. For the first term, we need $$\Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0}$$:

$$
\Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0} = \Gamma^0_{00} + \Gamma^j_{j0} \tag{60}
$$

From Blog 17, equations (31) and (32):

$$
\Gamma^0_{00} = \dot{\phi}, \quad \Gamma^j_{j0} = 3\left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a} - \dot{\phi}\right) = 3H - 3\dot{\phi} \tag{61}
$$

Summing these:

$$
\Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0} = \dot{\phi} + 3H - 3\dot{\phi} = 3H - 2\dot{\phi} \tag{62}
$$

When multiplied by $$T^{0i}$$ (which is first order), the term $$-2\dot{\phi}$$ becomes second order (product of two first-order quantities) and is discarded. Thus, to linear order:

$$
\Gamma^\mu_{\mu 0}T^{0i} \approx 3H \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}v^i}{a} \tag{63}
$$

### The Christoffel Curvature Term $$\Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda}$$

This term represents the "force" from spacetime curvature acting on the stress-energy:

$$
\Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} = \Gamma^i_{00}T^{00} + \Gamma^i_{0j}T^{0j} + \Gamma^i_{j0}T^{j0} + \Gamma^i_{jk}T^{jk} \tag{64}
$$

The last term involves $$T^{jk} \sim v^j v^k$$, which is second order, so we drop it.

For the first term, $$\Gamma^i_{00}$$ from Blog 17 equation (32):

$$
\Gamma^i_{00} = \frac{1}{a^2}\partial_i\phi \tag{65}
$$

And $$T^{00} = \rho(u^0)^2 \approx \rho(1 + 2\phi) \approx \bar{\rho}$$ to zeroth order in the multiplication (since $$\Gamma^i_{00}$$ is already first order). Thus:

$$
\Gamma^i_{00}T^{00} \approx \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a^2}\partial_i\phi \tag{66}
$$

For the remaining terms, $$\Gamma^i_{0j}$$ from Blog 17:

$$
\Gamma^i_{0j} = \frac{\dot{a}}{a}\delta^i_j - \dot{\phi}\delta^i_j = H\delta^i_j - \dot{\phi}\delta^i_j \tag{67}
$$

Since $$T^{0j} = \bar{\rho}v^j/a$$ is first order, and $$\dot{\phi}$$ is first order, the product $$\dot{\phi}T^{0j}$$ is second order and discarded. Thus:

$$
\Gamma^i_{0j}T^{0j} \approx H\delta^i_j \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}v^j}{a} = \frac{H\bar{\rho}v^i}{a} \tag{68}
$$

Similarly, because Christoffel symbols are symmetric in the lower indices ($$\Gamma^i_{j0} = \Gamma^i_{0j}$$):

$$
\Gamma^i_{j0}T^{j0} = \Gamma^i_{0j}T^{0j} \approx \frac{H\bar{\rho}v^i}{a} \tag{69}
$$

Summing the contributions to this term:

$$
\Gamma^i_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} \approx \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a^2}\partial_i\phi + \frac{2H\bar{\rho}v^i}{a} \tag{70}
$$

## Assembling the Euler Equation

Now we combine all terms in equation (49), multiplying through by $$a/\bar{\rho}$$ to simplify:

From equation (56): $$\dot{v}^i - 4Hv^i$$

From equation (63): $$+3Hv^i$$

From equation (70): $$+\frac{1}{a}\partial_i\phi + 2Hv^i$$

Summing these:

$$
\dot{v}^i - 4Hv^i + 3Hv^i + \frac{1}{a}\partial_i\phi + 2Hv^i = 0 \tag{71}
$$

The Hubble terms combine as $$-4H + 3H + 2H = +H$$, yielding:

$$
\dot{v}^i + Hv^i + \frac{1}{a}\partial_i\phi = 0 \tag{72}
$$

Rearranging to the standard form:

$$
\boxed{\dot{v}^i + Hv^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi} \tag{73}
$$

This is the **Euler equation** for CDM in an expanding universe. It states that the peculiar acceleration ($$\dot{v}^i$$) plus the Hubble drag ($$Hv^i$$) equals the negative gradient of the gravitational potential (scaled by the inverse scale factor to account for comoving coordinates).

## The Velocity Divergence Form

In cosmological perturbation theory, we often work with the **velocity divergence** $$\theta \equiv \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \partial_i v^i$$ rather than the vector field itself. Taking the divergence of equation (73):

$$
\partial_i\dot{v}^i + H\partial_i v^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial_i\partial^i\phi \tag{74}
$$

Assuming the order of differentiation commutes:

$$
\dot{\theta} + H\theta = -\frac{\nabla^2\phi}{a} \tag{75}
$$

where $$\nabla^2 = \partial_i\partial^i$$ is the Laplacian with respect to comoving coordinates.

In Fourier space, where $$\nabla^2 \to -k^2$$, this becomes:

$$
\dot{\theta} + H\theta = \frac{k^2}{a}\phi \tag{76}
$$

Note the sign: in gravitational wells where $$\phi &lt; 0$$, the right-hand side is negative, indicating that overdensities ($$\delta &gt; 0$$) induce infall ($$\theta &lt; 0$$), consistent with gravitational collapse.

## Coupling with the Continuity Equation

Together with the continuity equation from Blog 17:

$$
\dot{\delta} + \theta - 3\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{77}
$$

the Euler equation forms a closed system describing the evolution of density and velocity perturbations in CDM. These two equations, coupled with the Einstein equations (which relate $$\phi$$ to $$\delta$$ via the Poisson equation), completely determine the linear growth of structure.

In the next installment, we will solve this coupled system to find the **growth factor** $$D(a)$$, which tells us how primordial fluctuations seeded in the early universe grow into the large-scale structure we observe today.

## Summary

Beginning from the spatial components of the conservation equation $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu i} = 0$$, and carefully expanding each term using the established 4-velocity components and Christoffel symbols, we have derived:

$$
\boxed{\dot{v}^i + Hv^i = -\frac{1}{a}\partial^i\phi} \tag{78}
$$

or equivalently for the velocity divergence:

$$
\boxed{\dot{\theta} + H\theta = -\frac{\nabla^2\phi}{a}} \tag{79}
$$

These equations capture the competition between gravitational infall (the potential gradient) and cosmic expansion (the Hubble drag). They are the dynamical engine that drives structure formation, transforming the static gravitational potential wells into flowing rivers of dark matter that eventually coalesce into galaxies.
