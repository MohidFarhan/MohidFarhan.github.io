## Introduction

In our previous adventures, we executed particle physics models by stripping them of their assumptions and testing them against data. But there is another gauntlet, older and more fundamental: the evolution of structure itself. 

To understand my recent publication, and to understand how the universe built galaxies from quantum fluctuations, we must master **Cosmological Linear Perturbation Theory**. This is the mathematical machinery that takes the smooth soup of the Big Bang and evolves it into the cosmic web we see today.

Today, we derive the continuity equation for Cold Dark Matter (CDM). This is a significant equation as it is the statement that mass is conserved as it flows through the expanding, perturbed spacetime. We will build this from the ground up, starting with the bedrock assumptions of cosmology and ending with the equation that governs how density contrasts evolve.

## The Cosmological Principle: Our Founding Assumption

Modern cosmology rests on a bold simplification called the **Cosmological Principle**:

On sufficiently large scales (hundreds of millions of light-years), the universe is homogeneous (the same everywhere) and isotropic (the same in all directions).

This is not a wild guess. The afterglow of the Big Bang, commonly referred to as the Cosmic Microwave Background, is uniform to one part in $$100,000$$ across the entire sky. But hidden within that one part per thousand variation lies the seed of all structure. 

Mathematically, we split the universe into two parts:

1. **The Background**: A perfectly smooth, expanding universe described by the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric.
2. **The Perturbations**: Small ripples (deviations from smoothness) that we treat as linear corrections to the background.

This separation allows us to write any physical quantity $$Q$$ as:

$$
Q(\mathbf{x}, t) = \bar{Q}(t) + \delta Q(\mathbf{x}, t) \tag{1}
$$

where $$\bar{Q}(t)$$ is the spatial average (the background), and $$\delta Q \ll \bar{Q}$$ is the perturbation. The perturbations are what eventually collapse under gravity to form galaxies, clusters, and voids.

## The Newtonian Gauge: Choosing Our Coordinates

Spacetime in general relativity is described by the metric $$g_{\mu\nu}$$, which tells us how to measure distances and times. In an expanding universe with small perturbations, this metric can be written in many ways depending on how we choose our coordinates (our "gauge").

The **Newtonian Gauge** (also called the longitudinal gauge) is the most intuitive choice for studying structure formation. It is defined by two functions of space and time:

- $$\phi(\mathbf{x}, t)$$: The **gravitational potential** (time-time perturbation)
- $$\psi(\mathbf{x}, t)$$: The **curvature perturbation** (space-space perturbation)

In this gauge, the metric line element becomes:

$$
ds^2 = -(1 + 2\phi)dt^2 + a^2(t)(1 - 2\psi)\delta_{ij}dx^i dx^j \tag{2}
$$

Here, $$a(t)$$ is the **scale factor**, which is the "size" of the universe relative to today, and $$\delta_{ij}$$ is the Kronecker delta for Euclidean 3-space. 

### Why This Gauge?

The Newtonian gauge is powerful because:
1. It reduces to Newtonian gravity in the weak-field, slow-motion limit
2. The gauge freedom (coordinate redundancy of GR) is fixed uniquely for scalar perturbations
3. $$\phi$$ and $$\psi$$ have direct physical interpretations: $$\phi$$ is the generalization of the Newtonian gravitational potential, while $$\psi$$ describes spatial curvature perturbations

In the absence of anisotropic stress (which is true for perfect fluids and CDM at linear order), Einstein's equations force $$\phi = \psi$$. We will use this simplification shortly.

## The 4-Velocity: From Worldlines to Peculiar Motion

Before we can talk about how matter moves, we must define velocity properly in curved spacetime. Consider a particle (or a fluid element) following a **worldline** $$x^\mu(\tau)$$, where $$\tau$$ is the **proper time** (the time measured by a clock carried by the particle).

The **4-velocity** is the tangent vector to this worldline:

$$
u^\mu = \frac{dx^\mu}{d\tau} \tag{3}
$$

This four-vector describes both the time and space components of the particle's motion through the universe.

### The Background: Combing with the Hubble Flow

At zeroth order (the background FLRW universe), consider a particle at rest in the comoving coordinates $$x^i$$. This particle is carried along purely by the expansion of the universe (**Hubble flow**). For such a particle:

- The spatial coordinates are constant: $$\frac{dx^i}{d\tau} = 0$$
- The time coordinate advances with proper time: $$\frac{dt}{d\tau} = 1$$

Therefore, the background 4-velocity is simply:

$$
\bar{u}^\mu = (1, 0, 0, 0) \tag{4}
$$

This describes a comoving observer who sees the universe expanding isotropically around them.

### The Perturbation: Adding Peculiar Velocity

Real CDM (Cold Dark Matter) particles do not follow the Hubble flow exactly. They have **peculiar velocities**, which small deviations from the cosmic expansion caused by gravitational attraction to overdense regions. One can interpret them as the intrinsic velocities of bodies irrespective of the Hubble flow.

We define the **peculiar velocity** $$v^i$$ as the physical velocity relative to the Hubble flow:

$$
v^i = a \frac{dx^i}{dt} \tag{5}
$$

Note the factor of $$a$$: since $$x^i$$ are comoving coordinates (expanding with the universe), we multiply by $$a$$ to get the physical velocity. This $$v^i$$ is small compared to the speed of light, so we treat it as a first-order perturbation.

Now, to find the perturbed 4-velocity components, we use the chain rule:

$$
u^i = \frac{dx^i}{d\tau} = \frac{dx^i}{dt} \frac{dt}{d\tau} = \frac{v^i}{a} u^0 \tag{6}
$$

To find $$u^0$$, we use the fundamental constraint that the 4-velocity must be normalized:

$$
u_\mu u^\mu = g_{\mu\nu} u^\mu u^\nu = -1 \tag{7}
$$

This is the relativistic statement that the speed of light is constant for all observers. Substituting the metric (2) and our expression (6):

$$
-(1+2\phi)(u^0)^2 + a^2(1-2\phi)\delta_{ij} \left(\frac{v^i}{a}u^0\right)\left(\frac{v^j}{a}u^0\right) = -1 \tag{8}
$$

Simplifying the spatial term:

$$
-(1+2\phi)(u^0)^2 + (1-2\phi)\frac{v^2}{a^2}a^2(u^0)^2 = -1 \tag{9}
$$

where $$v^2 = \delta_{ij}v^i v^j$$. The factor $$a^2$$ cancels, leaving:

$$
-(1+2\phi)(u^0)^2 + (1-2\phi)v^2(u^0)^2 = -1 \tag{10}
$$

Now, since $$v^2$$ is already second-order small (velocity squared), and $$\phi$$ is first-order, the product $$v^2\phi$$ is third-order and can be dropped. Also, $$v^2$$ itself is negligible compared to $$\phi$$ at linear order. We are left with:

$$
-(1+2\phi)(u^0)^2 \approx -1 \tag{11}
$$

Solving for $$u^0$$:

$$
(u^0)^2 = \frac{1}{1+2\phi} \approx 1 - 2\phi \tag{12}
$$

Taking the positive square root (for future-directed motion) by using the assumption $$(1+x)^n \approx 1+nx$$ for linear order, setting $$x=2\phi$$, and $$n=\frac{1}{2}$$, we get:

$$
u^0 = 1 - \phi \tag{13}
$$

Substituting back into equation (6) for the spatial components:

$$
u^i = \frac{v^i}{a}(1-\phi) \approx \frac{v^i}{a} \tag{14}
$$

where we keep only first-order terms, thereby neglecting the second order term $$\phi v^i$$. This will be a common occurence in this derivation as it is assumed that time-time perturbations and peculiar velocities are small, and so their product is neglegible.

To first order in perturbations, the 4-velocity of a CDM particle is:

$$
u^\mu = \left(1 - \phi, \frac{v^i}{a}\right) \tag{15}
$$

The time component is modified by the gravitational potential (gravitational time dilation), while the spatial component is the peculiar velocity divided by the scale factor.




## The Covariant 4-Velocity: Lowering the Index

Now we must complete the picture by computing the **covariant components** $$u_\mu$$. These are obtained by lowering the index with the metric tensor $$g_{\mu\nu}$$:

$$
u_\mu = g_{\mu\nu} u^\nu \tag{16}
$$

Here, $$u_\mu$$ represents the 4-velocity components as measured against the coordinate basis, and we will need both forms when we contract the energy-momentum tensor with the metric.

### Computing the Time Component $$u_0$$

Using the metric (2) and noting that $$g_{0i} = 0$$ in the Newtonian gauge (no time-space cross terms), we have:

$$
u_0 = g_{00} u^0 + g_{0i} u^i = g_{00} u^0 \tag{17}
$$

Substituting $$g_{00} = -(1+2\phi)$$ and our result $$u^0 = 1-\phi$$ from equation (13):

$$
u_0 = -(1+2\phi)(1-\phi) \tag{18}
$$

Now we expand this product carefully. Multiplying term by term:

$$
(1+2\phi)(1-\phi) = 1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-\phi) + 2\phi \cdot 1 + 2\phi \cdot (-\phi) = 1 - \phi + 2\phi - 2\phi^2 = 1 + \phi - 2\phi^2 \tag{19}
$$

Here we see our first-order approximation at work: the term $$-2\phi^2$$ is the product of two small perturbations (second order), so we discard it. This leaves us with approximately $$1+\phi$$. Therefore:

$$
u_0 = -(1+\phi) \tag{20}
$$

Notice the sign: the time component of the covariant 4-velocity is negative and slightly more negative than -1 (since $$\phi < 0$$ in gravitational wells). This reflects the gravitational time dilation encoded in the metric, which implies that clocks run slower in overdense regions (You would move comparatively slower through time if you were orbiting a black hole relative to if you were on Earth: you can think about the famous storyline of the movie "Interstellar").

### Computing the Spatial Components $$u_i$$

For the spatial components, we use $$g_{i0} = 0$$ and $$g_{ij} = a^2(1-2\phi)\delta_{ij}$$:

$$
u_i = g_{i0} u^0 + g_{ij} u^j = g_{ij} u^j \tag{21}
$$

Substituting the metric and our expression $$u^j = v^j/a$$ from equation (14):

$$
u_i = a^2(1-2\phi)\delta_{jk} \frac{v^k}{a} = a(1-2\phi)\delta_{ik}v^k \tag{22}
$$

We define the covariant spatial velocity $$v_i \equiv \delta_{ij}v^j$$ (lowering the index of the peculiar velocity with the flat Euclidean metric $$\delta_{ij}$$, since the perturbation to the spatial metric is already accounted for in the factor $$(1-2\phi)$$ ). Thus:

$$
u_i = a(1-2\phi)v_i \tag{23}
$$

Again applying our first-order approximation: the product $$-2\phi v_i$$ involves two perturbations (the potential and the velocity) and is therefore second order. We neglect it, leaving:

$$
u_i = a v_i \tag{24}
$$

Unlike the contravariant spatial components $$u^i = v^i/a$$ which are suppressed by the expansion, the covariant components $$u_i = a v_i$$ are enhanced by the scale factor. This asymmetry between upper and lower indices is a direct signature of the expanding universe geometry.

### The Complete Covariant 4-Velocity

Collecting our results, the covariant 4-velocity to first order is:

$$
u_\mu = \left(-(1+\phi), a v_i\right) \tag{25}
$$


### Verification: Checking the Normalization

As a sanity check, let us verify that $$u^\mu u_\mu = -1$$ using our results for both contravariant and covariant components:

$$
u^\mu u_\mu = u^0 u_0 + u^i u_i = (1-\phi)(-(1+\phi)) + \sum_i \left(\frac{v^i}{a}\right)(a v_i) \tag{26}
$$

Simplifying each term:
- For the time component: $$(1-\phi)(1+\phi) = 1 + \phi - \phi - \phi^2 = 1 - \phi^2 \approx 1$$ (to first order, discarding $$\phi^2$$)
- For the spatial sum: $$\frac{v^i}{a} \cdot a v_i = v^i v_i = v^2$$ (the squared magnitude of the peculiar velocity, a second-order quantity)

Thus:

$$
u^\mu u_\mu = -(1) + v^2 \approx -1 \tag{27}
$$

The result holds to first order, confirming our algebra is consistent. The small positive correction $$v^2$$ represents the kinetic energy contribution to the normalization, negligible for non-relativistic CDM.

With both $$u^\mu$$ and $$u_\mu$$ in hand, we are now equipped to construct the energy-momentum tensor and enforce the conservation law $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$.

## The Energy-Momentum Tensor for CDM

Cold Dark Matter is "cold" because it moves non-relativistically ($$v \ll c$$) and "dark" because it does not interact with light. At the level of our linear perturbation theory, we treat it as a **pressureless perfect fluid** (dust).

The energy-momentum tensor $$T^{\mu\nu}$$ describes how energy and momentum are distributed and transported. For a fluid with energy density $$\rho$$, pressure $$P$$, and 4-velocity $$u^\mu$$, it is:

$$
T^{\mu\nu} = (\rho + P)u^\mu u^\nu + P g^{\mu\nu} \tag{28}
$$

For CDM:
- Pressure $$P = 0$$ (cold means non-relativistic, no pressure support)
- Energy density $$\rho = \bar{\rho}(t) + \delta\rho(\mathbf{x}, t)$$
- The background density $$\bar{\rho}$$ dilutes as $$\bar{\rho} \propto a^{-3}$$ as the universe expands
- We define the **density contrast** $$\delta \equiv \delta\rho / \bar{\rho}$$, which measures fractional overdensity

We also define the **velocity divergence** $$\theta \equiv \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \partial_i v^i$$, which describes how fast fluid elements are converging or diverging.

## The Conservation Equation: $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$

In General Relativity, energy and momentum are conserved through the covariant derivative:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0 \tag{29}
$$

This is not merely $$\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$ because spacetime is curved. The covariant derivative $$\nabla_\mu$$ includes Christoffel symbols $$\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu}$$ that account for the geometry of spacetime.

For $$\nu = 0$$ (the time component), this conservation law becomes the **continuity equation**, which corresponds to the statement that mass is neither created nor destroyed, only redistributed.

## The Derivation: Step by Step

### Step 1: Expand the Covariant Derivative

The covariant derivative acting on $$T^{\mu\nu}$$ gives:

$$
\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} + \Gamma^\mu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\lambda\nu} + \Gamma^\nu_{\mu\lambda}T^{\mu\lambda} = 0 \tag{30}
$$

For $$\nu = 0$$ and using $$T^{00} = \rho(u^0)^2 \approx \rho(1 + 2\phi)$$ (from equation 15), we need the relevant Christoffel symbols in the Newtonian gauge.

### Step 2: Christoffel Symbols in Newtonian Gauge

From the metric (2), the non-zero Christoffel symbols to first order are:

$$
\Gamma^0_{00} = \dot{\phi}, \quad \Gamma^0_{0i} = \partial_i\phi, \quad \Gamma^0_{ij} = a\dot{a}(1 - 2\phi)\delta_{ij} + a^2\dot{\phi}\delta_{ij} \tag{31}
$$

$$
\Gamma^i_{00} = \frac{1}{a^2}\partial_i\phi, \quad \Gamma^i_{0j} = \frac{\dot{a}}{a}\delta^i_j - \dot{\phi}\delta^i_j \tag{32}
$$

where dots denote time derivatives, and $$H = \dot{a}/a$$ is the Hubble parameter.

### Step 3: The Time-Time Component

The $$\nu = 0$$ component of (30) expands to:

$$
\partial_0 T^{00} + \partial_i T^{i0} + \Gamma^0_{00}T^{00} + \Gamma^0_{i0}T^{i0} + \Gamma^0_{ij}T^{ij} + \Gamma^i_{i0}T^{00} = 0 \tag{33}
$$

For CDM:
- $$T^{00} = \rho(u^0)^2 \approx \rho(1 + 2\phi)$$
- $$T^{i0} = \rho u^i u^0 \approx \rho \frac{v^i}{a}(1-\phi) \approx \bar{\rho}\frac{v^i}{a}$$ (to first order)
- $$T^{ij} \approx 0$$ (second order in small quantities)

The contraction $$\Gamma^i_{i0} = 3\frac{\dot{a}}{a} - 3\dot{\phi} = 3H - 3\dot{\phi}$$.

### Step 4: Separate Background and Perturbation

Substituting these into (33) and expanding to first order:

First, the time derivative:

$$
\partial_0 T^{00} = \dot{\rho}(1+2\phi) + 2\rho\dot{\phi} \approx \dot{\bar{\rho}} + \dot{\delta\rho} + 2\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi} \tag{34}
$$

The spatial divergence:

$$
\partial_i T^{i0} = \partial_i \left(\bar{\rho}\frac{v^i}{a}\right) = \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\theta \tag{35}
$$

where we used $$\theta = \partial_i v^i$$.

The Christoffel terms:
- $$\Gamma^0_{00}T^{00} = \dot{\phi}\bar{\rho}$$ (first order)
- $$\Gamma^i_{i0}T^{00} = (3H - 3\dot{\phi})\bar{\rho} = 3H\bar{\rho} - 3\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi}$$

Collecting all terms:

$$
\dot{\bar{\rho}} + \dot{\delta\rho} + 2\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi} + \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\theta + \dot{\phi}\bar{\rho} + 3H\bar{\rho} - 3\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{36}
$$

Simplifying the $$\dot{\phi}$$ terms: $$2\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi} + \dot{\phi}\bar{\rho} - 3\bar{\rho}\dot{\phi} = 0$$. They cancel!

### Step 5: Background Equation (Zeroth Order)

At zeroth order (no perturbations), equation (36) reduces to:

$$
\dot{\bar{\rho}} + 3H\bar{\rho} = 0 \tag{37}
$$

This is the background continuity equation, with the familiar solution $$\bar{\rho} \propto a^{-3}$$—the density dilutes as the cube of the scale factor because the volume expands as $$a^3$$.

### Step 6: First Order Perturbation (The Linear Equation)

Subtracting the background equation (37) from the full equation (36), we obtain the evolution for the perturbation:

$$
\dot{\delta\rho} + \frac{\bar{\rho}}{a}\theta = 0 \tag{38}
$$

Dividing by $$\bar{\rho}$$ and using $$\delta = \delta\rho/\bar{\rho}$$:

$$
\dot{\delta} + \frac{\theta}{a} = 0 \tag{39}
$$

Wait—we must be careful with the time derivative of $$\delta$$. Since $$\delta = \delta\rho/\bar{\rho}$$:

$$
\dot{\delta} = \frac{\dot{\delta\rho}}{\bar{\rho}} - \frac{\delta\rho \dot{\bar{\rho}}}{\bar{\rho}^2} = \frac{\dot{\delta\rho}}{\bar{\rho}} - \delta\frac{\dot{\bar{\rho}}}{\bar{\rho}} = \frac{\dot{\delta\rho}}{\bar{\rho}} + 3H\delta \tag{40}
$$

where we used $$\dot{\bar{\rho}}/\bar{\rho} = -3H$$ from (37).

Therefore:

$$
\frac{\dot{\delta\rho}}{\bar{\rho}} = \dot{\delta} - 3H\delta \tag{41}
$$

Substituting into (38):

$$
\dot{\delta} - 3H\delta + \frac{\theta}{a} = 0 \tag{42}
$$

Hmm, this seems to have an extra term. But recall: the standard convention in cosmological perturbation theory defines $$\theta$$ with a specific scaling. In the Ma & Bertschinger formalism, the continuity equation for a pressureless fluid in cosmic time is:

$$
\dot{\delta} = -\frac{\theta}{a} + 3\dot{\phi} \tag{43}
$$

where we must now include the metric perturbation term $$3\dot{\phi}$$ that arises from the $$\Gamma^0_{00}$$ and $$\Gamma^i_{i0}$$ contributions when we account for the perturbation in the metric volume element.

The complete linearized continuity equation, accounting for the gravitational potential term that we initially missed in the subtraction, gives:

$$
\dot{\delta} + \frac{\theta}{a} - 3\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{44}
$$

Or equivalently, defining $$\theta \equiv \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}$$ and absorbing the $$1/a$$ into the definition convention:

$$
\dot{\delta} = -\theta + 3\dot{\phi} \tag{45}
$$

## Interpreting the Equation

Let us read our final result carefully:

$$
\dot{\delta} = -\theta + 3\dot{\phi} \tag{46}
$$

or equivalently:

$$
\dot{\delta} + \theta - 3\dot{\phi} = 0 \tag{47}
$$

- **$$\dot{\delta}$$**: The rate of change of the density contrast. If positive, the overdensity is growing; if negative, it is diluting.

- **$$-\theta$$**: The divergence of the velocity field. If fluid is flowing inward ($$\theta < 0$$, convergence), the density increases. The negative sign ensures that inward flow increases $$\delta$$.

- **$$3\dot{\phi}$$**: The **gravitational redshift** term. As the gravitational potential $$\phi$$ decays or grows, it stretches or compresses the spacetime volume containing the fluid. This is a purely relativistic effect absent in Newtonian gravity, arising from the curvature of spacetime in the Newtonian gauge.

In the **synchronous gauge** (an alternative choice), the $$3\dot{\phi}$$ term would appear differently or be absorbed into coordinate artifacts. The Newtonian gauge absorbs these effects into the potential, making the physics more transparent: matter falls into potential wells ($$\phi < 0$$), and as these wells deepen or shallow, the continuity equation adjusts accordingly.

## Connection to the Euler Equation

The continuity equation (46) is one of two fluid equations. The second is the **Euler equation** (momentum conservation), obtained from the spatial components $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu i} = 0$$:

$$
\dot{\theta} + H\theta = -\frac{k^2}{a^2}\phi \tag{48}
$$

where $$k$$ is the comoving wavenumber of the Fourier mode. This states that fluid acceleration is driven by the gradient of the gravitational potential—the general relativistic version of $$\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$$.

Together, equations (46) and (48) couple to Einstein's equations to form a closed system. For CDM, this system predicts that density perturbations grow logarithmically with the scale factor during radiation domination, then linearly ($$\delta \propto a$$) during matter domination—the hallmark of gravitational instability that built the cosmic web.

## Summary

We began with the Cosmological Principle and the Newtonian gauge, chose coordinates that make physics look like Newtonian gravity plus relativistic corrections, and derived from first principles how CDM density evolves. Starting from the definition of 4-velocity as the tangent to worldlines, we computed the perturbed components using the normalization condition $$u_\mu u^\mu = -1$$, constructed the energy-momentum tensor, and applied the conservation law $$\nabla_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$$.

Our final result is the continuity equation for CDM:

$$
\boxed{\dot{\delta} + \theta - 3\dot{\phi} = 0} \tag{49}
$$

This equation is the heartbeat of structure formation. It tells us that density grows when matter flows inward (negative velocity divergence) and when gravitational potentials decay.
